Two Kinds of Events

There are kinds of events that we consider. In the first, all of the particles are charged. In the second, one of the particles (either the initial particle or one of the decay products) is neutral. Our approach to analyzing these two kinds of events is a bit different.

If all of the particles in an event are charged, they will all leave circular tracks in the detector and we will be able to calculate the momentum vectors for all of the particles. In this case, momentum conservation can be checked in the and directions by subtracting the total final momentum from the initial momentum.

If one of the particles is neutral, we can determine the and components of the momentum of the neutral particle (which escapes the detector undetected) by first calculating the momentum of each of the charged particles and then using the conservation of momentum.

Sample Analysis: K- → X+ + Y- + π-

Let's consider the decay K- → X+ + Y- + π-, a decay for which all of the particles are charged. (We make some comments below about how things change if there is a neutral particle in the decay.)

The goal of our analysis is to determine the identities of the unknown particles in the decay (X+ and Y-) and to determine the energies and momenta of all of the particles.

We can proceed in a similar manner for the other charged particles in the event. Recalling that the rotation direction of the particle's trajectory is determined by its charge (positive or negative), we can immediately determine which of the outgoing paths corresponds to the X+ (since only one of the outgoing paths is counter-clockwise). Beyond this, however, we have some ambiguity. In particular, we have two problems:

  1. We don't know which of the two negatively charged particles (going clockwise) is the π- and which is the Y-. We can determine the momentum for each track (from the track's radius), but we don't know the particles' identities, so we can't calculate the energy with confidence.
  2. We don't know the identities of the X+ and Y- particles. Again, this means that we can't compute their energies with certainty.

Fortunately, there are only a finite number of possibilities for X+ and Y-. To see what these are, you can look at the list of particle decays. The only decays that match the signature K- → X+ + Y- + π- are:

  • K- → e+ + e- + π-
  • K- → μ+ + μ- + π-
  • K- → π+ + π- + π-

Thus, there are a total of five different configurations to check (three possibilities for X and Y, plus two ambiguities in the final state for e- ↔ π- and μ- ↔ π-). To determine the identities of the particles, we need to try out the various options and see which is most consistent with the conservation of energy. If you use a spreadsheet for your analysis you will see that it is not difficult to check out the various options.

Using a "guess and check" approach, we would eventually see that the underlying decay is K- → e+ + e- + π- in this case. Our final results may be summarized in a table as follows:

...where the last column shows β = v/c = p/E, which is the speed of the particle expressed as a fraction of the speed of light. We have kept some extra significant digits for β for the electron and positron, so that you can see that they are going very close to (but not quite at!) the speed of light.

In the second-to-last row of the table we have shown the disagreement between the initial energy and momentum and the final values (this is "Δ" in each case). If energy and momentum were conserved exactly in our analysis, then this row would contain zeros. Our "measurements" of the particles' energies and momenta have included small uncertainties, however, so this has led to an apparent non-conservation of energy and momentum. To determine "Δ%," we have divided Δ by the magnitude of the largest entry in the corresponding column, and multiplied the result by 100%. As you can check, the other possible choices for particle assignments give significantly worse agreement for the conservation of energy (i.e., a larger value for the corresponding Δ).

Data Analysis for Decays Involving a Neutral Particle

In the example described above, all of the particles were charged, so they all left a track in the wire chamber, allowing their momenta to be measured. If the decay includes one neutral particle, one can use the conservation of momentum to determine the and components of the momentum of the neutral particle. In this case, there would be no "Δ" or "Δ%" entries for the momentum columns, since the results would simply be zero.
